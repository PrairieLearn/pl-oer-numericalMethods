Recall that a function $f(x)$ can be expressed as a Taylor Series centered at a number $x_0$: $$ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(x_0)}{n!}(x-x_0)^n $$

which can be truncated at any integer $N$ to approximate $f(x)$ with varying levels of accuracy. Usually, the closer $x$ is to $x_0$, the better the truncated series approximates $f(x)$.

Below is a plot of $\sin(x)$, $f(x) = x$, and the $n=3$ Taylor series expansion of $\sin(x)$.

Often $f(x) = x$ is used to approximate $\sin(x)$. You can verify that this is the first nonzero term of the Taylor series of $\sin(x)$ about $x_0 = 0$. This estimate is valid only in a small neighborhood near $x=0$. We want to examine its behavior at points progressively further from the center of the Taylor series for a particular value of $\hat{x} = 0.7$:

  • Calculate the $n = 1$ and $n = 3$ Taylor series expansions of $\sin(x)$ about $x_0 = 0$ evaluated at $\hat{x}$.
  • Calculate the $n = 3$ Taylor series expansion of $\sin(x)$ about $x_0 = \frac{\pi}{4}$ evaluated at $\hat{x}$.
  • Calculate the true value of $\sin(\hat{x})$. Use this value to calculate the relative errors of the approximations. Save the results in err_0_1, err_0_3, and err_pi4_3.

Remember that the formula for relative error is $\left|\frac{y_{approx} - y_{true}}{y_{true}}\right|$.
