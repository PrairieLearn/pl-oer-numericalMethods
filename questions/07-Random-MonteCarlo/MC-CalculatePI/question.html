Problem Setup

The Monte Carlo method is commonly used to approximate areas or volumes of shapes. In this problem, we will use Monte Carlo to find the area of a circle, allowing us to estimate the value of $\pi$. Furthermore, we will use several different sample sizes to see how the accuracy increases with larger samples.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monte_Carlo_method

Implementation

Part 1

According to the formula $A = \pi r^2$, for a circle of radius 1, the area of the circle will be equal to $\pi$. Therefore, if we can use Monte Carlo to approximate the area of the circle, we can find an estimate for $\pi$.

Write a function calculate_pi that accepts a sample of x and corresponding y coordinates as arguments and returns an estimate for $\pi$ (since we want to calculate $\pi$ for various sample sizes, it will be useful for it to be a function). To simplify things, we will only consider a single quadrant of the unit circle (see gif above). Therefore, the area of the full circle will be 4 times the area we calculate for the single quadrant (and your function should take this into account).

Part 2

Using the calculate_pi function you wrote in Part 1, estimate the value of $\pi$ for different sample size of powers of 10 ranging from $10^0$ to $10^6$ (inclusive). Store these values in an array of size 7 named pi. The samples has been given to you in xs and ys. For example, if you need 10 samples, you may use xs[:10] to get the x coordinates of these 10 samples.

Part 3

Plot the absolute error of your approximations vs. the sample size on a log-log plot. The error can be obtained by subtracting the true value of $\pi$ from your approximations. Provide appropriate axes labels and a title. Save your plot to a variable plot.

You should notice that the error decreases with larger sample sizes, although there may be a considerable amount of noise due to randomization. The order of convergence of Monte Carlo is $O\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\right)$, so the slope of your plot should be around $-1/2$.
